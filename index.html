Acne is a prevalent skin condition that significantly impacts physical and psychological well-being, often leading to anxiety, low self-esteem, and depression. To address the challenges of accurately diagnosing and treating various acne types, we propose AcneIntelliScan, a comprehensive AI-driven solution. This system comprises four key components: first, it utilizes machine learning, deep learning, and image processing techniques to classify different types of acne; second, it incorporates Explainable AI (XAI) to generate visual explanations, enhancing transparency in decision-making; third, it addresses data limitations by generating a diverse dataset of synthetic acne images using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs); and fourth, it employs object detection and segmentation methods to assess acne severity for personalized treatment decisions. This integrated approach not only improves classification and severity assessment but also enriches dermatological datasets, contributing to the continuous advancement of dermatological diagnostics and treatment strategies.

LITERATURE SURVEY

The diagnosis of acne is traditionally reliant on dermatologists, which often results in extended wait times and high treatment costs. Factors like gender, age, and skin type play a crucial role in treatment plans, and while individuals can observe symptoms such as comedones and pustules, there is no definitive test for acne. Recent advancements in computer vision and machine learning have facilitated automated systems for acne classification, moving beyond traditional methods like Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and Random Forests. Studies have demonstrated the use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for acne lesion classification, leveraging deep learning to automate feature extraction and improve accuracy. However, many traditional approaches still require significant manual effort and struggle to generalize across varied datasets.

The emergence of Vision Transformers (ViTs) has introduced new methodologies for image classification, demonstrating their potential in medical image analysis. While research on ViTs has shown promise in classifying skin lesions and addressing class imbalances, specific applications for acne classification, particularly using smartphone images, remain limited. Notably, several studies have attempted to classify acne types using deep learning models, yet many have faced challenges due to the restricted nature of their datasets and the complexities inherent in differentiating between acne types. Additionally, while some studies have utilized advanced models like GANs for synthetic data generation to address dataset limitations, they often overlook the nuanced variations in acne morphology.

Despite the advancements in machine learning and computer vision, significant gaps exist in the accurate classification and grading of acne severity. Existing systems often struggle with transparency in decision-making and lack the granularity required for clinical practice. Traditional grading systems, such as the Global Acne Grading System (GAGS), while widely used, do not always provide the needed detail for effective treatment. This research aims to explore advanced architectures like YOLO models for simultaneous object detection and segmentation to enhance diagnostic precision and develop a novel grading system.

Moreover, the significance of developing a comprehensive solution for acne diagnosis cannot be overstated. Acne is a prevalent skin condition with profound physical and psychological impacts, contributing to anxiety, depression, and lowered self-esteem. Traditional diagnostic methods, heavily reliant on dermatologists, can result in delays and increased costs, exacerbating patients' emotional distress. The existence of Computer-Aided Diagnosis (CAD) systems for acne represents a transformative breakthrough in addressing these challenges. However, many existing systems rely on simple CNN models, which struggle to discern subtle differences between similar acne types and often lack transparency in their decision-making processes. This research proposes an Advanced Digital Acne Type Diagnostic System, employing advanced machine learning techniques for versatile acne classification and transparent decision-making through Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI), synthetic data generation, and acne severity detection.

The XAI component of this study addresses the challenge of the "black box" nature of complex classification models, providing visual explanations of the model's decisions through heat maps. These heat maps highlight regions of the skin that the model focused on during classification, offering both clinicians and patients insights into the diagnostic process. This transparency not only aids in confirming the acne diagnosis but also enhances user trust in the system. The methodology involves collecting a diverse dataset of acne images, preprocessing them, and integrating advanced XAI techniques to generate accurate and interpretable heat maps.

By addressing these challenges and leveraging cutting-edge technology, this research aims to improve acne diagnosis and treatment outcomes in dermatological care.

METHODOLOGY

This section highlights the methodologies used for each component of this study on diagnosing and classifying acne vulgaris through advanced machine learning technologies. The research addresses specific challenges in acne diagnosis through various techniques and models.

Following that, four subcomponents are discussed:

  1. Acne Classification
  2. Transparency in Decision-Making
  3. Data Diversity
  4. Severity Assessment


Acne Classification This component identifies and categorizes different types of acne using an ensemble approach that combines Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Vision Transformers (ViTs), and Support Vector Machines (SVMs). The process begins with user-uploaded selfies, verified for facial presence using OpenCV's Haar Cascade. Various CNN architectures, including DenseNet and Xception, were tested, with Xception achieving the highest accuracy of 90% after hyperparameter tuning. The study also explored ViT models, focusing on transfer learning, with the ViT_base_16 model achieving 81% accuracy. For traditional machine learning, SVM and Random Forest models were evaluated, with SVM selected for its slightly higher accuracy of 77.5%. The final ensemble model employs a majority voting mechanism for classification, incorporating dynamic user input to enhance diagnostic accuracy.

Transparency in Decision-Making This subcomponent enhances understanding of the diagnosis through visual explanations. By employing techniques such as Grad-CAM and LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), the system provides users and clinicians with insights into the reasoning behind the model's predictions, fostering trust and clarity in the diagnostic process.

Data Diversity To improve accuracy, this component creates a comprehensive dataset of various acne images. This dataset consists of 1,023 images of five acne types, collected from various online sources and validated by a clinical consultant. To standardize the images, they were resized to 299x299 pixels, and five augmentation techniques were applied to increase the dataset size to 1,628 images. Essential preprocessing techniques, such as normalization and resizing, were employed to prepare the dataset for training within deep learning frameworks.

Severity Assessment This component evaluates acne severity for tailored treatment recommendations by leveraging YOLO models to identify acne lesions based on precision, recall, and mean average precision metrics. An integrated grading system classifies acne severity while employing segmentation techniques for accurate delineation of lesions. The results facilitate detailed severity reports generated by OpenAI tools, offering actionable insights for clinicians.

The results of our evaluations will assess the accuracy and effectiveness of the machine learning models proposed in our research. We will examine the performance of each model across various metrics and scenarios. Our discussions will describe the main findings and implications of our research, highlighting strengths, limitations, and potential avenues for future exploration.

RESEARCH PROBLEM

Acne vulgaris is a prevalent skin condition impacting many individuals globally, necessitating accurate classification for effective treatment. Traditional diagnostic methods often rely on subjective assessments by dermatologists, leading to inconsistencies. Current research predominantly focuses on the severity of acne rather than identifying specific types, which is essential for tailored treatment approaches. This project proposes an innovative ensemble model that combines various technologies to improve the accuracy and generalizability of acne type classification, thereby addressing existing gaps in the literature.

The management of acne is crucial to prevent progression and minimize complications, yet conventional diagnostic methods face significant barriers, including prolonged wait times and high consultation costs. Existing approaches often lack precision, relying on subjective evaluations that may overlook variations in acne presentation. The proposed Advanced Digital Acne Type Diagnostic System aims to enhance severity assessment through advanced object detection and segmentation techniques. By leveraging machine learning and computer vision technologies, this system seeks to provide a nuanced understanding of acne manifestations, enabling more effective treatment planning and improved patient outcomes.

Despite advancements in Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) for acne diagnosis, a critical gap exists in incorporating Visual Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) to enhance transparency in decision-making processes. The research identifies specific problems, such as the interpretability gap in DNNs and the limited focus on acne type classification. By addressing these challenges, the project aims to develop an Advanced Digital Acne Type Diagnostic System that utilizes machine learning and XAI to provide a comprehensive, transparent, and user-friendly acne diagnostic solution.

The development of a robust Synthetic Data Generation Pipeline is essential to overcome challenges related to the limited availability and diversity of clinical image datasets for acne diagnosis. Current methods often fail to capture the complexity of acne variations and may not represent the full spectrum observed in practice. This research aims to create a pipeline leveraging Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion models to generate diverse and realistic acne images. Additionally, establishing standardized evaluation metrics and ensuring the clinical authenticity of generated images through external verification will enhance the quality and usability of the synthetic dataset for research and diagnostic purposes.

RESEARCH GAP

Acne diagnosis and classification face significant challenges, including the reliance on subjective assessments by dermatologists, long wait times, and high costs, which limit accessibility to effective treatment. Current methodologies often focus on acne severity rather than precise type detection, particularly in selfie images, leading to gaps in the use of machine learning models like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and vision transformers for accurate classification. This study proposes an ensemble approach integrating these technologies alongside a synthetic image generation pipeline to diversify training datasets and enhance robustness in acne type detection. Additionally, it addresses shortcomings in acne severity assessment by comparing traditional grading systems with advanced YOLO architectures for simultaneous detection and segmentation. The study further emphasizes the importance of incorporating Visual Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) to improve interpretability and user trust in automated tools, focusing on generating accurate heat maps for classification. Ultimately, the proposed solutions aim to enhance diagnostic accuracy, address privacy concerns regarding data sharing, and establish standardized evaluation metrics, improving the overall effectiveness of acne diagnosis in clinical settings.
